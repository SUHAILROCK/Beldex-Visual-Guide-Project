Chapter 01

What is actually
happening when you
send a message.

When you tap Send on WhatsApp, your message does not simply travel from your phone to your friend's phone. It travels to a Meta server in California, where it is decrypted, scanned for compliance and advertising signals, re-encrypted, and then forwarded. The metadata who you contacted, when, how often, from what location is permanently logged.

Telegram is more nuanced. Its standard chats are stored on Telegram's servers in plaintext. "Secret chats" are end-to-end encrypted, but the app still knows your phone number, device fingerprint, IP address, and contact list. If Telegram's servers were compromised, your social graph would be exposed.

Even Signal the gold standard of consumer privacy requires a phone number. That phone number links your Signal identity to a real-world identity, and Signal knows when you joined, when you were last active, and the phone numbers of the people you talk to.

BChat is architecturally different. There is no central server. There is no company database of user accounts. The app does not know who you are because it never asked. There is genuinely nothing to breach, because nothing is stored.

Chapter 02

Your identity on BChat
does not exist until
it is generated locally.

Creating an account on BChat is radically different from every other messaging app. There is no form to fill out. No phone number verification. No email confirmation. You tap one button, and your phone's cryptographic hardware generates a complete identity without contacting any server.

What gets created is an Ed25519 keypair an asymmetric cryptographic key pair used across blockchain systems worldwide. The public key becomes your BChat ID a 64-66 character alphanumeric string. The private key never leaves your device.

Simultaneously, a 24-word mnemonic recovery seed is derived from your private key. This is the only way to restore access to your account on a new device. It is your responsibility to store it safely BChat cannot help you if it is lost, because BChat does not know it.

Your BChat ID is also automatically linked to a unique Beldex wallet address. This means every BChat account has built-in BDX payment capability without any separate registration your messaging identity and your payment identity are one and the same.

Chapter 03

The encryption engine:
every message gets
a brand new key.

BChat uses the TextSecure protocol the same cryptographic foundation used by Signal. At its core is the Double Ratchet Algorithm combined with Extended Triple Diffie-Hellman (X3DH) for initial key agreement. This combination provides something called Perfect Forward Secrecy.

Perfect Forward Secrecy means that even if an attacker records all your encrypted messages today and later steals your private key, they still cannot decrypt those past messages. This is because each message is encrypted with a unique, ephemeral key that is deleted after use.

The "ratchet" metaphor is intentional: just like a ratchet wrench that only turns one way, the key state advances forward with each message and can never go backwards. An attacker who breaks key N cannot derive key N-1 or any previous key.

When two BChat users first contact each other, their apps perform an X3DH handshake a multi-step key exchange that establishes a shared secret without ever sending that secret across the network. From that shared secret, the Double Ratchet begins generating an endless chain of message keys.

Three layers of encryption in every message

Message Encryption
AES-256-CBC + HMAC-SHA256
The message content is encrypted with a symmetric key derived from the Double Ratchet chain. Each message has a unique key, generated from the previous key plus a ratchet step. Deleting a message truly removes access there is no way to recover keys that have been ratcheted past.
Session Encryption
Curve25519 + X3DH
The session layer establishes the shared secret between two parties using Curve25519 elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman. The X3DH handshake uses four different keys: your identity key, a signed prekey, a one-time prekey, and an ephemeral key. This multi-key exchange makes it mathematically impractical to forge a session.
Transport Encryption
Onion Layers (per-hop)
On top of the end-to-end encrypted message, the network adds per-hop encryption for each masternode in the onion route. This transport encryption ensures that no node can see the plaintext of the inner layers, even if the E2E encryption were somehow compromised.
Double Ratchet how each message key is different

Each arrow represents one ratchet step. The key used for Message 5 is cryptographically derived from Message 4's key but you cannot reverse that process to get Message 4's key from Message 5's key. Each key is also deleted immediately after use.

Chapter 04

Onion routing: why no
one can trace where
your message came from.

Encryption alone is not enough to protect you. Even if an encrypted message cannot be read, it can still be tracked. A surveillance system watching your internet traffic knows which IP addresses your device communicates with and from that it can infer who you are talking to, when, and how frequently.

BChat solves this with onion routing a technique originally developed for the Tor anonymity network and adapted here for the Beldex masternode infrastructure. Before your message enters the network, it is wrapped in three nested encryption layers, one for each node in the route.

Think of it as a series of envelopes. The outer envelope is addressed to Node 1. Inside is another envelope addressed to Node 2. Inside that is a final envelope addressed to Node 3, which then delivers the innermost message to the recipient.

Crucially, each node only ever sees the outermost envelope. Node 1 knows your IP address but not the destination. Node 3 knows the destination but not your IP address. No single node and no collaboration between nodes can link sender and recipient.

The four encryption layers in detail

L0
Your Message
The original E2E encrypted payload. Encrypted with the recipient's public key. Readable only by the final recipient with their private key.
L1
Node 3 Wrap
L0 is wrapped with Node 3's public key + delivery instructions for the final hop. Node 3 decrypts this layer and sees only the delivery address.
L2
Node 2 Wrap
L1 is wrapped with Node 2's key + instructions to pass to Node 3. Node 2 decrypts this and knows only: "send what's inside to Node 3."
L3
Node 1 Wrap (outermost)
L2 is wrapped with Node 1's key. Your device sends this outer layer. Node 1 sees your IP address but cannot see any inner content or destination.

Why not just use Tor?

Tor uses volunteer exit nodes that can be operated by anyone, including adversaries. Beldex masternodes require operators to lock 10,000 BDX as collateral creating a strong economic disincentive to operate malicious nodes. A majority attack on the routing network would require acquiring and locking hundreds of millions of dollars in BDX.

Chapter 05

Swarm storage: how
your message waits
without being read.

One practical challenge for decentralised messaging is offline delivery. In a traditional messenger, if you are offline, your messages sit on a central server until you reconnect. But BChat has no central server. How does it hold your messages?

The answer is Swarm Storage. When a message is sent to a BChat ID whose owner is currently offline, the exit node (Node 3) forwards the encrypted message to a group of masternodes called a swarm a cluster of 8-10 masternodes that are jointly responsible for storing messages for a particular BChat ID range.

The swarm stores only the encrypted message ciphertext. The masternodes in the swarm have no ability to read it they do not have the recipient's private key. They are functioning as encrypted storage boxes, holding sealed envelopes they cannot open.

When the recipient comes online, their BChat app contacts the swarm and retrieves all pending encrypted messages. The messages are decrypted locally on the recipient's device. After retrieval, messages are deleted from the swarm. No permanent storage of your communications exists anywhere on the network.

Step 01 / Sent
Message enters the route
Your device wraps the encrypted message in three onion layers and sends it through Node 1. It travels through Node 2, then to Node 3 (exit). The recipient is offline at this moment.
Step 02 / Stored
Swarm accepts the payload
Node 3 identifies the swarm responsible for the recipient's BChat ID and forwards the fully encrypted message. 8-10 masternodes store identical copies for redundancy no single node can drop your message.
Step 03 / Delivered
Recipient retrieves and decrypts
When the recipient comes online, their app polls the swarm, retrieves the ciphertext, and decrypts it locally. The swarm then deletes the message. The network retains no copy of what was said.
Key guarantee

Because every masternode in the swarm receives only the already-encrypted ciphertext, a complete compromise of every masternode in a swarm would reveal nothing readable. Decryption requires the recipient's private key, which is stored only on the recipient's device and never transmitted to any network node.

Chapter 07

Private calls: peer-to-peer,
no phone number,
encrypted in transit.

BChat supports voice and video calls without requiring a phone number, an account on a call provider, or any subscription service. Calls are established directly between two devices using WebRTC the same technology used by video conferencing tools but with end-to-end encryption layered on top.

How Private Calls Work Technical Stack
🤝
Call Setup via BChat ID

Calls are initiated using the recipient's BChat ID. No phone number dialling. The call request is sent through the normal BChat message routing to the recipient.

BChat ID → masternode route → recipient device
🔐
DTLS-SRTP Encryption

Audio and video data streams are encrypted using DTLS-SRTP the standard used by WebRTC. All media packets are encrypted before transmission and cannot be intercepted in transit.

DTLS 1.2 + SRTP (AES-128 / AES-256)
📡
ICE and STUN for Direct Connection

BChat uses the ICE (Interactive Connectivity Establishment) framework with STUN servers to punch through NAT firewalls and establish a direct peer-to-peer connection where possible. The call payload never passes through Beldex's infrastructure once connected.

WebRTC ICE + STUN / TURN fallback
🚫
Message Request Gate

You cannot receive calls from people you have not accepted a message request from. This prevents unsolicited call spam and ensures you are always in control of who can reach you.

Message request must be accepted before call access
⚙
Calls Can Be Disabled

For users who only want text messaging or who use BChat in professional settings where calls would be disruptive the calling feature can be completely disabled in app settings. When disabled, no call requests are received at all.

Settings → Privacy → Disable Voice and Video Calls
Step-by-step: How a private call is established
01
Alice taps "Call" on Bob's BChat ID

BChat generates a SDP (Session Description Protocol) offer containing Alice's connection capabilities. This is encrypted and sent to Bob via the normal BChat message pathway.

02
Bob receives the encrypted call request

Bob's device decrypts the call offer. A notification appears. Bob's identity is verified through the BChat ID keypair Alice knows with certainty she is calling the right person.

03
ICE candidate exchange and DTLS handshake

Both devices exchange network candidates (IP addresses and ports) to find the best direct connection path. DTLS performs a handshake to establish encryption keys for the media stream.

04
Peer-to-peer encrypted stream begins

Audio and video flow directly between devices, encrypted with SRTP. The Beldex network is no longer involved the call is a direct encrypted tunnel. No server can record or intercept it.

05
Call ends no recording, no logs

BChat does not record calls. No call log is stored on any server. The session keys are discarded. The only record of the call is on the devices of the two participants, locally.

Chapter 08

In-chat payments: send money
as privately as you
send a message.

BChat has a full Beldex wallet integrated directly into every conversation. Because every BChat ID is already linked to a Beldex wallet address at account creation, payments require no setup and no additional registration. And because Beldex uses three distinct privacy technologies, the payment itself is as private as the message that accompanies it.

How payment privacy works
🔁
Ring Signatures who sent it?

Your transaction is signed alongside 10 other real transactions ("decoy outputs") drawn from the blockchain. An observer sees 11 possible senders and cannot determine which one actually sent the payment.

RingCT with 10 decoys per transaction
📫
Stealth Addresses who received it?

For each incoming payment, your wallet generates a unique one-time address derived from your public key. The transaction is sent to this ephemeral address. Only you can identify it as belonging to you.

Elliptic Curve Diffie-Hellman key derivation
🔒
RingCT how much was sent?

Transaction amounts are hidden using Pedersen Commitments a cryptographic proof that the input amounts equal the output amounts without revealing what those amounts actually are. Bulletproofs+ reduces proof size by 80%.

Pedersen Commitments + Bulletproofs+
⚡
Flash Transactions instant settlement

BDX uses Flash for instant transaction confirmation. The 0.003 BDX fee is flat regardless of amount sending 10 BDX costs the same as sending 10,000,000 BDX. Cross-border, instant, private.

0.003 BDX flat fee · ~2 minute confirmation

Traditional bank transfers and crypto networks charge fees proportional to the transaction amount or to network congestion.
